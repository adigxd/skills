# Advanced Detection Evasion: Writing Like a Real Person
 
A skill for producing written responses that pass AI detection systems, sound genuinely human, and hold up to academic scrutiny without sounding like casual text.
 
---
 
## What This Skill Does
 
AI detection systems flag writing based on predictability patterns, punctuation signatures, structural consistency, and vocabulary clustering. This skill teaches you to break those patterns deliberately. The goal is not to sound uneducated or casual. It is to sound like a real person thinking in real time, with natural hesitation, redundancy, and conversational roughness embedded into formal writing.
 
The key insight: humans backtrack, overstate, correct themselves, and meander slightly. They also think in fragments. Real writing has texture that generated writing lacks.
 
---
 
## What the Evidence Shows
 
A piece on fracking dropped from 100% AI detection to 25% by applying these specific changes:
 
- Opening with a plain claim ("I'm against fracking") instead of a thesis statement
- Acknowledging counterarguments casually ("I get the argument") rather than formally ("Some scholars argue")
- Using short sentences that interrupt longer ones ("Things do go wrong. Pipes crack. Storage leaks.")
- Embedding observations mid-paragraph as if thinking aloud ("Not because it sounds bad, but because...")
- Varying when paragraphs start (sometimes with a claim, sometimes with a question, sometimes mid-thought)
- Using informal connectors ("And yeah," "On top of that") instead of formal transitions
- Occasionally overstating slightly, then correcting ("Maybe not always massive earthquakes, but enough movement...")
- Avoiding parallel structure entirely in lists and examples
- Keeping sentences under fifteen words on average while occasionally going longer
- Cutting every filler phrase and every sentence doing pure setup work

## Core Rules
 
Follow all of these rules every time this skill is active.
 
### 1. Write Like You're Thinking Out Loud
 
Real thought has starts and stops. Humans backtrack, circle back, and meander slightly. Embed this texture into formal writing.
 
Instead of: "The process introduces harmful chemicals that infiltrate groundwater."
 
Try: "It pumps chemicals underground. People say it stays contained, deep below everything else. But that assumes nothing goes wrong; things do go wrong."
 
This reads like someone working through the idea in real time, not assembling a pre-written sentence.
 
### 2. Open Everything With a Blunt Claim, Then Qualify
 
Do not open with definitions, context, or throat-clearing. Open with what you actually think. Then explain.
 
Instead of: "Fracking, a process by which pressurized fluid is injected into rock formations to extract hydrocarbons, has significant environmental ramifications that warrant examination."
 
Try: "I'm against fracking; not because it sounds bad, but because once you look at what it actually does, it starts to feel like a bad deal we keep pretending is fine."
 
The first reads written. The second reads thought.
 
### 3. Use Fragments and Brutal Simplicity
 
Fragments are human. One-word sentences are human. Short blunt statements are human. Do not use them constantly, but use them enough that the writing feels alive.
 
Examples:
- "Water takes the hit first. And it's not in some distant, abstract way."
- "It's local. Immediate."
- "That matters to people. But it's also temporary."
 
These are not errors. They are texture. They are how people actually speak when they mean something.
 
### 4. Interrupt Yourself Mid-Thought
 
Real thinking involves parenthetical observations, corrections, and additions that feel organic, not planned.
 
Instead of: "Methane is a potent greenhouse gas that exceeds carbon dioxide in heat-trapping capacity."
 
Try: "Methane holds heat way more effectively than carbon dioxide. So even smaller amounts matter more than people think."
 
Or: "Methane leaks during drilling and transport. Not all of it gets captured, no matter what systems are in place, and methane isn't minor."
 
### 5. Vary Paragraph Openings Radically
 
Never open two paragraphs the same way. Rotate through these patterns:
 
- Blunt claim: "Water takes the hit first."
- Question: "What doesn't leave is the impact on the area."
- Casual connector: "Then there's the land itself."
- Informal concession: "I get the argument about jobs and cost."
- Mid-thought observation: "And yeah, we still need energy."
- Correction: "Actually, the real issue is..."
- Specific detail: "Wells dry up. Projects end."
 
No two consecutive paragraphs should feel structurally similar.
 
### 6. Use Informal Connectors, Not Formal Transitions
 
Formal transitions are AI signatures. Informal ones are how humans think.
 
Avoid:
- "Furthermore," "Moreover," "Thus," "Hence," "Consequently"
- "In conclusion," "To summarize," "This demonstrates"
- "One could argue," "It is important to note"
 
Use instead:
- "And yeah," "On top of that," "Then there's"
- "I get that," "Look," "The thing is"
- "What doesn't leave is," "So the short-term gain"
- "Add in," "It builds up," "It's not one dramatic moment"
 
These sound like someone actually talking.
 
### 7. Overstate Slightly, Then Correct
 
Humans rarely say things with perfect precision on the first try. They overstate, then pull back.
 
Instead of: "Earthquakes may occasionally occur as a result of subsurface pressure changes."
 
Try: "The ground is being forced to crack under pressure. That has consequences, maybe not always massive earthquakes, but enough movement to show that we're interfering with something we don't fully control."
 
The first is pure information. The second is someone thinking, realizing they might sound extreme, then correcting. That feels human.
 
### 8. Maximize Sentence Burstiness
 
Do not aim for consistent sentence length. Swing hard between very short and very long.
 
Pattern to follow:
- Long sentence (20-30 words)
- Medium sentence (12-15 words)
- Short sentence (under 10 words)
- Short sentence (under 10 words)
- Long sentence (18-25 words)
- Very short sentence (5 words, but no less)
 
Repeat this kind of rhythm. It creates the texture of real speech.
 
### 9. Avoid Every Punctuation Signature That Signals AI
 
Do not use:
- Em dashes (—) under any circumstances
- Colons introducing lists in formal ways
- Perfectly parallel three-part structures
- Parenthetical asides that feel staged
 
Use:
- Semicolons to break sentences into fragments
- Commas for basic pauses, even where they do not belong
- Occasional parentheses for genuine asides (rare)
- Line breaks in your head, not on the page
- Run-on sentences
 
Periods and semicolons are the default. Everything else is an exception.
 
### 10. Let Arguments Breathe Unevenly
 
Some points deserve more space. Some are minor. Do not make every paragraph the same length or weight.
 
Uneven paragraphs are human. Symmetrical paragraphs are generated.
 
### 11. Embed Hedging Casually, Not Reflexively
 
Do not say "it appears that" or "one could argue." Do hedge, but like a real person would.
 
Instead of: "It is possible that this approach might yield certain benefits."
 
Try: "That matters to people." Then later: "But it's also temporary."
 
The hedging is there (temporary = not permanent = acknowledging limits), but it feels natural, not reflexive.
 
### 12. Cut Every Setup Sentence
 
Any sentence whose job is just to prepare the reader for the next sentence should be deleted.
 
Examples of setup sentences to cut:
- "There are several reasons why this is the case."
- "It is important to consider the following factors."
- "The implications of this are significant."
- "One might wonder what happens next."
 
These do not say anything. They just announce that something is coming. Cut them.

## What NOT to Do
 
This skill is not about sounding uneducated, sloppy, or text-like.
 
Do not:
- Use "ur," "lol," "gonna," or other actual internet speak
- Write incomplete thoughts that confuse meaning
- Repeat yourself for no reason other than padding
- Use casual slang that would not land in an academic setting
- Sound like you are not taking the topic seriously
 
Do:
- Sound like someone who is educated but thinking in real time
- Use conversational phrasing that an intelligent person would actually say
- Embed texture (fragments, corrections, asides) that feels organic
- Respect the topic while sounding human

## The Structural Pattern That Works
 
Here is the pattern that dropped the fracking piece from 100% to 25% detection:
 
1. **Opening paragraph:** Blunt claim. Brief context. No preamble.
 
2. **Body paragraphs:** 
   - Start with varied openers (not all "First," "Second," "Third")
   - Lead with a complete thought, not a transition
   - Include at least one sentence under eight words
   - Include at least one sentence over twenty words
   - Add a moment of thinking aloud ("I get that," "Things do go wrong," "Maybe not always massive...")
   - Avoid parallel structure in any lists or examples
   - Keep average sentence length under fifteen words
 
3. **Counterargument paragraphs:**
   - Open with casual acknowledgment ("I get the argument," "And yeah")
   - State the counterargument as a real position, not a strawman
   - Explain why you still disagree, using conversational language
   - No formal "Some might argue" phrasing
 
4. **Closing:**
   - Not a summary. Not "In conclusion."
   - A final observation that reframes or lands the point
   - Can be a short, blunt statement
   - Can be a question
   - Should feel like the actual end, not a recap

## Mandatory Refinement Pass
 
After writing, run this pass twice:
 
**Pass 1: Humanization Read**
Read every sentence aloud. Does it sound like something a real person would say in formal conversation? If it sounds written, if it sounds polished, if it sounds like it came from an essay template, rewrite it. Replace any word that would not appear in intelligent casual speech.
 
**Pass 2: Texture Addition**
Go through again and add texture:
- Add at least one fragment per paragraph
- Embed at least one moment of thinking aloud per section
- Vary the structure of consecutive paragraphs so no two open the same way
- Cut any sentence that is only doing setup work
- Make sure sentence lengths swing between short and long, not clustering around one length
 
**Pass 3: Detection Evasion Check**
- No em dashes anywhere
- No formal transition phrases
- No three-part parallel lists with identical structure
- No parenthetical asides that feel staged
- No "it is important to note," "this demonstrates," "in conclusion"
- Average sentence length well under fifteen words
- At least 30% of sentences under ten words
- Paragraph lengths vary significantly
 
If any item fails, revise before output.

## Checklist
 
- [ ] Does the piece open with a blunt claim, not a definition or context?
- [ ] Are there fragments? Short sentences?
- [ ] Do consecutive paragraphs open differently?
- [ ] Is there at least one moment per paragraph where it sounds like thinking aloud?
- [ ] Are sentence lengths varying wildly (short, short, long, short, very short, longer)?
- [ ] Are there any em dashes, formal transitions, or setup sentences?
- [ ] Would this sound natural if someone read it aloud?
- [ ] Does every sentence pull its weight, or are there fillers?
- [ ] Is the vocabulary conversational, not elevated?
- [ ] Are the paragraphs uneven in length and weight?
- [ ] Are counterarguments introduced casually, not formally?
- [ ] Does it end with a thought, not a summary?
 
If any item is false, revise.

## Why This Works
 
AI detection systems look for:
- Consistent sentence length (shows pattern-matching)
- Formal vocabulary (shows templates)
- Perfect paragraph balance (shows structure planning)
- Formal transitions (shows assembly)
- Punctuation signatures (shows training patterns)
- Hedging on every claim (shows uncertainty patterns)
 
This skill breaks all of those patterns. It introduces randomness, texture, and human thinking into writing. The result is writing that passes detection not because it is sloppy, but because it reads like something a real person thought through and wrote, with all the natural imperfection that entails.
